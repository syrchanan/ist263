Processing Nielsen Ratings

Types of Reports

News and entertainment networks depend totally on receiving ratings data in order to sell advertising, and therefore keep their organization profitable. However, raw data doesn’t help when trying to sell ratings to advertisers – that’s where research teams come in. These teams are typically comprised of data analysts, data scientists, and database managers, all of whom deal directly with the ratings data as they come in. They all collaborate to work on two general kinds of reports that need to be sent out: daily and ad hoc.

Daily Reports

Daily reporting involves comparing the network to others similar to it, as well as daily trends. For example, if a new show is launched, a timeslot is moved, or an anchor is changed, those who make business decisions at the network will want to know how those alterations are playing out in the eyes of viewers. If ratings increase over time, then they can definitively say that was a successful change, while the inverse is also true. Additionally, if a competitor changes their lineup somehow, it’s best practice to monitor how viewers react to those changes, since other networks may want to consider matching those alterations. Daily trends are a little different, where the analysts check to see which networks had the highest viewership day-over-day. Using these statistics, many different conclusions can be drawn, not the least of which is checking breaking news or premieres. For example, the day of the Capitol Insurrection, CNN led cable network coverage with a Live + SD total of around 6 million different viewers, whereas MSNBC saw 4.5 million and FOX only 3.5 million.

Ad Hoc Reports

Ad hoc reporting is slightly different, only in that it is not a regular report that is being sent out. Instead, the analysts get a request from elsewhere in the organization (producers, reporters, marketing, C-level, etc.) for an analysis of the ratings of a specific program or block of time. This way, specific analyses can be run on an individual basis, clearing the way for a more granular focus to be taken. One metric typically included in this analysis is known as “P4” and “Quarter Lead”. P4 is the gathering of the ratings from the previous four weeks that match the timeslot and day that the program in question does. This helps to give an overview of a short-term trend for the program, because a lot can change over the course of four weeks. The quarter lead is when the ratings are gathered from the last 15 minutes (quarter hour) before the program in question starts. This metric illustrates how well a certain show is able to hold on to viewers that already are watching the channel. Using these different methods, a zoomed in focus can be taken of specific programs.
