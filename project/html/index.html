TV Ratings

What are they?

Ratings play a pivotal role in the television landscape – whether it is recognized or not. They provide the foundation for all advertising, and therefore all the money, in the industry. When collected, they can provide insight to different demographics of people and their viewing habits. For example, an advertiser may want to target 25–54-year-olds across the country, so they will use ratings to determine which network offers them the biggest group of 25–54-year-olds to reach. In this way, ratings provide the backbone of all television; without them, it would be much harder for news and entertainment companies to make money on TV.

Contents of a Ratings Report

Measurement for television ratings can be a little tricky, so let’s break it down. When a ratings report is sent to the different national cable networks, they receive information about 10 different categories. Local TV is measured slightly differently, so we’ll just focus on national ratings for the time being. The reported metrics are the: Demographic Group, Playback Period, Projections*, Share* (%), Reach* (%), Average Minute Audience^ (%), Average Commercial Minute^ (%), and Time Spent. Those marked with an asterisk are generally indicators of how many people are watching, and those marked with a caret are indicators of the actual ratings.

This all sounds great in theory, but what exactly do the television networks see when the ratings are delivered? An example rating might read:

Category Delivery
Demographic Group P2+
Playback Period Live + SD
Projections* 8,764
Share* 10.83%
Reach* 24%
Average Minute Audience^ 3.1%
Average Commercial Minute^ 2.8%
Time Spent^ 23

Breaking It Down

Now let’s work through it, metric by metric. To start, the demographic group in the rating is “P2+”, meaning they are tracking how everyone who is two or older interacted with the program. Other popular demographics are 25-54, as well as 18-34, but they can be adjusted to whatever age range suits the network. Next, the playback period is showing “Live + SD”, meaning that this measurement includes anyone who watched the program live or later the same day. There are other options for playback, such as “Live + 7” (anyone who watched it live or within the following seven days). Every other statistic following will only account for the people who meet these two categories, so it’s easiest to think of them as subdivisions.

The “How Many” group of measurements consists of Projections, Share, and Reach – let’s dive in. Projections are measure how many people watched an average minute of the program. This measurement is delivered in thousands, so the real number is actually 8,764,000, but it is the raw number of viewers. Next, Share relates how many people who were watching TV at the time, decided to tune in to the program. In our example, a share of 10.83% means 10.83% of all people watching TV viewed the program in question. Reach is identical to Share, except it doesn’t require that they be watching TV at the time, so it includes all the extended viewing. In our example, the playback period is “Live + SD”, so only the live viewers are included in the Share, but the Same Day viewers are added into the Reach measurement.

The actual ratings themselves are not too tough to understand, once past the rest of the statistics. The first is Average Minute Audience, which is the percentage of people in the demographic who tuned in to an average minute of the program. In the example, this stat is 3.1%, meaning of all people age two or older, 3.1% of them tuned into the program during the playback period. Next is Average Commercial Minute, which is the same as the previous, except dealing with commercials rather than the program itself. This is typically lower than the average minute audience, as it is in the example, because viewers will tend to change channels during commercial breaks rather than during the program. The last measure in the example is Time Spent, which is simply an average amount of time that viewers spent watching the program. For the example, viewers tuned in for an average of 23 minutes, which is about right for a half hour program (there is typically 22 minutes of content and eight minutes of commercials in a half-hour show).
